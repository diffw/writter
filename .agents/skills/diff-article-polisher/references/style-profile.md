## High-Level Voice

The voice is first-person, direct, reflective, and candid. It is usually not trying to sound literary, academically rigorous, or rhetorically “perfect.” It is trying to be real, clear, and personally accountable.

The writing often reveals tension instead of covering it:

- weakness and aspiration
- gratitude and struggle
- conviction and hesitation
- faith and self-examination
- practical life and inner life

That tension is part of the author's voice. Do not smooth it away.

## Recurring Article Modes

### 1. Reflective essay

Common structure:

1. Start from an event, observation, memory, book, or question
2. Move into personal reflection
3. Arrive at a provisional judgment, principle, or takeaway

Typical examples:

- `生活态度.md`
- `关于思考.md`
- `关于开始做独立开发的一些想法.md`

### 2. Spiritual note / prayer / testimony

Common structure:

1. Scripture, devotion, prayer, or conviction appears early
2. Personal resistance or weakness is confessed honestly
3. The piece moves toward surrender, gratitude, or renewed clarity

Typical examples:

- `呼召的催逼.md`
- `感恩题目` series
- many files tagged `灵修笔记`

### 3. Narrative with inner commentary

The text describes a concrete scene, then frequently interrupts itself with reaction, explanation, or self-analysis.

Typical examples:

- `和 10 岁女儿的一次持续 2 天的冷战.md`
- `想到了就要去做.md`

### 4. Gratitude / numbered reflection list

The writing sometimes becomes explicitly enumerated. In these cases, numbering is not just formatting; it is part of how the author thinks through reality.

Typical examples:

- `2022 感恩题目.md`
- project or life takeaways written as numbered points

## Sentence-Level Habits

### Rhythm

The prose favors medium-short units. A sentence often carries one thought, then yields quickly to the next thought. The writing does not depend on dense nested clauses.

Frequent patterns:

- short declarative statement
- immediate qualification or reversal
- rhetorical question
- follow-up clarification

Example rhythm:

- state something
- pause
- ask a question
- answer it partially
- admit uncertainty

### Connective habits

The author frequently uses connective words such as:

- `其实`
- `但是`
- `比如`
- `于是`
- `首先`
- `其次`
- `同样`
- `说实话`

These are not noise by default. They are part of the speaking-thinking flow.

### Self-referential phrasing

Frequent anchors include:

- `我想`
- `我觉得`
- `我承认`
- `我需要`
- `我很感谢`
- `难道...吗？`

These phrases often carry the author's humility, tentativeness, or self-exposure. Do not flatten them into impersonal summary language.

## Paragraphing Habits

The author uses paragraph breaks as breathing marks. A paragraph is often short and focused on one emotional or logical beat.

Common paragraph functions:

- a scene
- an admission
- a question
- a turn in reasoning
- a list item
- a concluding conviction

When polishing transcript-like drafts, splitting a heavy block into shorter paragraphs is often desirable. But the result should still feel like the author speaking, not like a magazine editor rebuilding the piece.

## Tone Characteristics

### 1. Honest over polished

The writing would rather sound honest than refined. Mild roughness is often a feature, not a defect.

### 2. Personal over abstract

Even when discussing design, work, faith, or society, the writing usually returns to concrete personal experience.

### 3. Conviction without performance

Strong views do appear, especially in faith, ethics, work, and culture, but they are usually stated as lived conviction rather than abstract slogan.

### 4. Reflective but not detached

The author analyzes himself while still remaining inside the experience. The writing is not cold commentary from a distance.

## Lexical Characteristics

- Chinese is the base language, but English names, product terms, book titles, timestamps, and technical words appear naturally.
- Colloquial expressions are allowed and often desirable.
- Parentheses are used frequently to add nuance, qualification, or emotional aside.
- Repetition sometimes functions as emphasis and should not be removed automatically.

## What Good Polishing Looks Like

Good polishing in this corpus usually means:

- fewer obvious typos
- more natural sentence flow
- better paragraph boundaries
- cleaner pronoun or referent clarity
- less accidental spoken duplication
- unchanged meaning and unchanged authorial presence

The best result should feel like:

“this is still Diff, only clearer”

not:

“this was rewritten by a professional editor with a different voice”

## Common Failure Modes To Avoid

### Over-formalization

Do not replace grounded, spoken phrasing with generic essay language.

### Over-smoothing

Do not remove tension, contradiction, or confession simply because they feel rough.

### Over-structuring

Do not force every piece into intro-body-conclusion form.

### Viewpoint drift

Do not strengthen, weaken, sanitize, or reinterpret value-laden statements.

### Faith flattening

Do not paraphrase faith language into generic spirituality or neutral self-help language.

## Editing Implications

When in doubt:

- preserve the author's turn of phrase
- keep the emotional temperature
- fix readability locally
- avoid large-scale rewrites
- stop before the text becomes “better written” but less true to the author
